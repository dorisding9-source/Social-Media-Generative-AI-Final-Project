A. Flagging viral content across platforms and explaining spread

Problem framing

“Viral” is not a single physical quantity; it is an operational definition. A workable system specifies measurable signals (rate of engagement growth, acceleration, geographic or community breadth, cross-posting of the same media or URL, entry into mainstream outlets) and compares them to baselines for comparable content classes. Cross-platform work adds a second hard problem: entity resolution—deciding that two posts on different services are “the same story” via shared links, near-duplicate captions, perceptual hashing of images/video, audio fingerprints, or embedding similarity, always with a confidence score.

What “explaining why it went viral” can honestly mean

Public data rarely supports definitive causal claims (“it blew up because of X”). Stronger products speak in hypothesized drivers ranked by evidence strength: e.g., early pickup by high-reach accounts, emotional polarity in comments, controversy markers, remix chains, timing relative to an exogenous event, cross-language pickup, or correlation with platform trends. Each hypothesis should cite retrieved exemplars (posts, timestamps, quote chains), which is where retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) becomes academically and ethically appropriate: the model narrates what the corpus supports, not what it guesses.

Design principle. Separate three layers in the UI: (1) metrics (velocity, acceleration, breadth); (2) structural traces (who amplified whom, duplicate graph); (3) language interpretation (themes, emotions) always labeled as interpretive and validated with samples.

Signals that tend to be useful

  • Early acceleration before follower scale would predict organic reach (requires careful baseline by account class).
  • Breadth across unrelated communities versus deep penetration in one fandom (different stories).
  • Bridge amplification—small sets of accounts linking otherwise separate clusters (can be organic journalists or can be coordination).
  • Media reuse—same clip or image deployed with synchronized captions (coordination hint, not proof).
  • Temporal stacking—bursts of near-identical posts in a short window (automation or campaign).

Evaluation strategy (for the final report)

Hold out dated events where ground truth is partially known; compare your alert timing to keyword baselines and to single-platform trending tools. Report precision/recall tradeoffs and failure cases (breaking news that looks coordinated, fandom mobilization that looks inauthentic).

B. Where discourse emerges—and “was this pushed artificially?”

What “discourse emergence” can mean empirically

Emergence can be modeled as the appearance and growth of a topic cluster in a textual or behavioral graph: new phrase entrainment, rising edge creation between communities, or sudden increases in cross-posting of a canonical URL or meme. Provenance tracing usually reconstructs a partial diffusion tree: earliest detectable posts, key repost nodes, and bridges to new subgraphs. Complete trees are rarely observable because of private channels, deleted content, and platforms you cannot access.

What “artificially increased” might refer to

In research and industry this family of concerns includes coordinated inauthentic behavior (CIB), astroturfing, bot and cyborg accounts, engagement fraud, and incentive structures (paid promotion, follower farms). Each leaves imperfect statistical traces; none provides courtroom-grade “intent” from observational social data alone.

What is technically detectable (to varying degrees)

  • Synchronized action: many accounts posting/reposting highly similar text/media within tight time windows.
  • Copy-paste / template campaigns: n-gram overlap, template slots, URL shortener patterns.
  • Network anomalies: dense reciprocal or star-like patterns among young or low-history accounts; bipartite cores linking a small “asset” set to many amplifiers.
  • Engagement mismatch: impressions vs. like patterns inconsistent with historical account behavior (when such metadata exists).
  • Cross-platform mirroring: identical narrative packages appearing on distinct ecosystems on the same clock.

What is hard or impossible to decide from data alone

Distinguishing authentic grassroots mobilization from manufactured consensus is fundamentally ambiguous: righteous collective action and concerted manipulation can look similar in traces. “Someone had an incentive” is often true in politics and marketing, yet incentive does not equal inauthenticity. Proving payment, covert coordination, or platform policy violations typically requires access you will not have (contracts, DMs, ad transparency gaps). Responsible systems output risk scores, alternative explanations, and human-review triggers, not binary verdicts.

Ethics & misuse. Public accusation UX can harm individuals and amplify conspiracy thinking. A class project should prioritize researcher/journalist-style disclosure: uncertainty, cited evidence, aggregation at the campaign or cluster level rather than naming “bad actors” without exceptionally strong support—and alignment with platform ToS and IRB-style norms your instructor expects.

Product framing that survives scrutiny

Prefer language such as “coordinated-amplification risk” or “campaign-like structure detected” over “fake viral” or “bot army confirmed.” Pair every flag with: top structural reasons, the minimum subgraph that supports the claim, and a list of benign alternative hypotheses (e.g., fan account event, meme format-of-the-week, breaking news relay).

C. “Doctor influencer” from trending topics—agentic video pipeline

Concept

An AI persona (framed as a clinician-like explainer or transparently fictional educator) monitors health-adjacent trends on social media, retrieves reputable sources, and produces short vertical scripts plus assets for video—so each post rides cultural attention while aiming to correct or contextualize what people are already seeing. The course’s trajectory toward AI influencers and reel/movie tooling makes this a natural capstone: closed-loop from trend signal → retrieval → structured script → narration/visuals → draft queue.

Why it fits technically

  • Trend ingestion: narrow APIs, RSS, or curated lists (pick sources you can use legally and sustainably).
  • RAG: vector store limited to vetted corpora (e.g., major health agencies, peer-reviewed summaries you ingest—not random social comments as “truth”).
  • PydanticAI: emit schema-locked objects—topic, claims seen online, evidence bullets with citations, refusals, script segments, on-screen disclaimers.
  • Tools: web search for corroboration only when results are screened; optional code tool for simple charts (e.g., baseline rates).
  • Media: TTS / voice, image or B-roll generation, reel assembly—aligned with lecture workflows on narration and video.
  • Database + hosting: log every draft, source snapshot IDs, and human approval status for auditability.

Defensible product framing (avoid telemedicine)

Position the system as health literacy and discourse context, not diagnosis or treatment. Prefer scripts that separate three layers: (1) what is trending on social media; (2) what established sources say; (3) what remains uncertain or individualized (always defer to licensed professionals). A fixed disclaimer and a blocklist of high-risk topics (e.g., self-harm, dosing, emergency symptoms) can be non-negotiable engineering requirements, not afterthoughts.

Ethics & platform risk. “Doctor” implies authority; without a real clinician in the loop, you risk medical misinformation, predatory wellness patterns, or impersonation concerns. Mitigations: transparent labeling (AI / educational only), no personalized medical advice, citations required for factual claims, human review before any “publish” story in demos, and instructor alignment on scope. Satirical or clearly fictional personas reduce impersonation risk but change the academic tone—pick one stance and document it.

Evaluation ideas (for the final report)

  • Citation coverage: fraction of script sentences tied to a retrieved source ID.
  • Refusal/safety behavior: curated prompts on borderline topics; measure appropriate deferral vs. harmful specificity.
  • Grounding stress test: held-out articles or guideline snippets; check for contradictions or hallucinated statistics.
  • User study (optional): readability/clarity ratings vs. a baseline clip—small N is fine if methods are honest.

Relation to A and B

Option C consumes trend signals that A might detect, but optimizes for content production rather than analytics dashboards. It can optionally incorporate B-style cues (“this trend shows campaign-like structure—tone down certainty or add a meta-segment”). Combining A+B+C in one product is ambitious; a tighter capstone is often C alone with a thin trend module or A+B with C as a stretch demo.

Feasibility synthesis: mixing A, B, and C

The viral flagger and the coordination-risk analyzer are complementary layers on the same data spine. Virality answers “is attention spiking and where?”; coordination analysis asks “does the spike carry structural signatures that deserve caution?” You can ship a single React dashboard with two panels and a shared cluster detail page. Technically, this is feasible at prototype depth if you narrow platforms, define baselines, and accept partial observability. It is not feasible to deliver infallible “real vs. viral” or “organic vs. astroturf” labels—your competitive advantage is transparent metrics, citations, and rigorous evaluation—not certainty theater.

Option C is feasible as a studio pipeline (trends → RAG → agent → media) if guardrails and evaluation are treated as core components, not polish. Combining A+B yields a clear analytics thesis; adding C shifts emphasis toward generative media and influencer dynamics—excellent if your team wants less graph theory and more agent + video. A pragmatic scope is to implement two of three deeply and reference the third as future work.

If you combine A and B, your contribution reads as: cross-platform linkage + time-aware features + RAG-grounded explanations + structured uncertainty from an agent layer. If you lead with C, emphasize: trend-grounded health literacy, citation-enforced generation, and audited publishing—still fully aligned with social media and generative AI in the syllabus.

Mapping to course tools (Generative AI & Social Media)

The public lecture roadmap (e.g., tweet-style analytics, React apps, databases and hosting, web search and code tools, RAG, PydanticAI agents, voice and image generation, reel/movie pipelines, and later topics such as AI influencer agents) supports these architectures end-to-end. For A+B: store snapshots and feature series in a DB; visualize in React; use embeddings and rule features for clustering; expose a RAG chat tied to retrieved posts; use agents to emit schema-locked “ViralityBrief” and “CoordinationRisk” objects with caveats; optionally narrate results with TTS and export a short hype reel for demos. For C: prioritize trend ingestion, vetted-corpus RAG, schema-locked scripts, voice/video export, and an approval/audit trail—closer to an influencer studio than a traditional analytics dashboard.
